What Is a Transformer, and Why Is It So Good?

The architecture that changed everything

Before 2017, AI processed language one word at a time. RNNs (Recurrent Neural Networks) passed a hidden state from word to word — like a game of telephone. By word 50, the signal from word 1 was mostly lost. LSTMs improved memory somewhat, but the fundamental problem remained: sequential processing meant no parallelism and weak long-range connections.

The Transformer, introduced in the 2017 paper "Attention Is All You Need", replaced all of this with a single mechanism: self-attention. Instead of processing words in order, it lets every word look at every other word simultaneously.

Self-attention in 30 seconds: Each word gets three vectors — a Query (what am I looking for?), a Key (what do I contain?), and a Value (what information do I carry?). The model computes Query × Key scores for every word pair, applies softmax to get weights, then takes a weighted sum of Values. Result: each word gets a new representation that incorporates context from the entire sentence — all computed in parallel.

In the sentence "The cat sat on the mat because it was tired" — self-attention lets "it" directly attend to "cat" with a high weight, regardless of distance. An RNN would have to carry that information through 6 intermediate steps, losing signal at each one.

This gave Transformers three superpowers that older models couldn't match:

1. Parallelism — All word pairs are computed at once on GPUs, making training orders of magnitude faster.

2. Long-range connections — Word 1 and word 500 are equally "close" to each other. No information loss over distance.

3. Scalability — The architecture scales predictably with more data and compute. This directly enabled Era 2.

Why Did GPT Win Over BERT?

Two approaches to the same architecture — one proved more versatile

Both GPT and BERT are built on the Transformer. But they use it in fundamentally different ways, and that difference determined which approach would dominate.

GPT

Generative Pre-trained Transformer
  • Reads left-to-right only (like reading a book)
  • Trained to predict the next word
  • Can generate text, code, conversations
  • One model does many tasks: write, translate, summarize, reason
  • Gets better at understanding through generation

BERT

Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers
  • Reads in all directions (sees full context)
  • Trained to fill in masked words
  • Cannot generate new text
  • Excellent at classification, search, understanding
  • Needs task-specific fine-tuning for each use
Why GPT won: Generation is a superset of understanding. If you can predict the next word well enough, you implicitly understand grammar, facts, reasoning, and style. GPT-style models turned out to be "universal task solvers" — give them instructions in plain text and they can do almost anything. BERT excels at specific tasks (search ranking, sentiment analysis), but it can't hold a conversation, write an essay, or reason through a problem. The future turned out to need generalists, not specialists.

What Are Scaling Laws?

The discovery that made "just make it bigger" a legitimate strategy

In 2020, researchers at OpenAI discovered something remarkable: model performance follows a predictable mathematical relationship with three variables — the number of parameters (model size), the amount of training data, and the compute budget. This wasn't a rough trend. It was a power law: performance improves as a smooth, predictable function of scale.

This meant companies could predict in advance how good a model would be before spending hundreds of millions training it. It turned AI research from "try things and hope" into an engineering problem: if you want X% better performance, invest Y more compute.

Model Size vs. Capability Over Time

The key insight: Scaling laws showed that making models bigger doesn't just add incremental improvement — it unlocks qualitatively new capabilities. GPT-2 could write paragraphs. GPT-3 could do few-shot learning (learn from 2-3 examples). GPT-4 could pass the bar exam and reason about images. Each jump in scale crossed a threshold where the model could suddenly do things it couldn't before.

What Is Reasoning? How Does It Work?

From "predict the next word" to "think through the problem"

Early LLMs would answer a math problem by jumping straight to the answer — and often getting it wrong. The breakthrough came in two stages: first Chain-of-Thought prompting, then reasoning models trained via RL.

Chain-of-Thought (2022) was deceptively simple: if you add "Let's think step by step" to a prompt, the model performs dramatically better on reasoning tasks. Why? Because each intermediate step becomes part of the context for the next prediction. The model is essentially giving itself scratch paper.

How reasoning models (o1, Claude Extended Thinking) go further:

Instead of just being prompted to think step by step, these models are trained via reinforcement learning to internalize the habit. During training, the model gets rewarded for correct final answers. Over thousands of iterations, it learns that decomposing problems, checking intermediate steps, and backtracking when something seems wrong leads to better rewards. The result: a model that automatically allocates more "thinking tokens" to harder problems — spending 10 tokens on "What's 2+2?" but 2,000 tokens working through a complex coding problem.

Importantly, reasoning is still "just" next-token prediction at the mechanism level — but the distribution of what gets predicted has been shaped by RL to favor systematic, step-by-step approaches. The model has learned that thinking before answering produces better outcomes, just like a human learns to show their work in math class.

How Do Agents Turn Text Into Actions?

From chatbot to autonomous worker

An LLM by itself can only output text. To make it actually do things — search the web, write files, call APIs — you need function calling and an agent loop.

Function calling works like this: the model is trained to recognize when it needs a tool, and instead of outputting regular text, it outputs a structured JSON command. A host program intercepts this, executes the real action, and feeds the result back to the model.

An AI agent takes this further by running in a loop. Instead of one question → one tool call → one answer, the agent autonomously decides what to do next, over and over, until the task is complete:

The critical difference from a chatbot: In a chatbot, the human drives the conversation — asking questions, deciding what to do next. In an agent, the AI drives the workflow — deciding which tools to use, in what order, handling errors, and self-correcting. The human provides the goal; the agent figures out the path.

The Major Model Families

A landscape of who's building what — and how they differ

The AI model landscape in 2025 is split between closed-source frontier models (accessible via API) and open-source models (downloadable and modifiable). Each family has different strengths, trade-offs, and philosophies.

Family Company Latest Approach Strengths
GPT OpenAI GPT-5 Closed Broad capabilities, multimodal, reasoning
Claude Anthropic Opus 4.6 Closed Safety, long-context, coding, agents
Gemini Google 3.1 Pro Closed 1M+ context, multimodal, reasoning
Llama Meta Llama 4 Open Open-source, MoE, 200 languages
Mistral Mistral AI Large 3 Mixed Speed, efficiency, European data sovereignty
DeepSeek DeepSeek V3 Open Cost-efficient ($6M training), near GPT-4
Qwen Alibaba Qwen 3 Open Multilingual, video understanding
The open vs. closed debate: Closed models (GPT, Claude, Gemini) tend to be the most capable and lead in safety research, but require API access and trusting the provider. Open models (Llama, DeepSeek, Qwen) let you run models on your own hardware, fine-tune for specific tasks, and maintain data sovereignty — but may lag in raw capability and safety guardrails. The gap is closing fast: DeepSeek V3 achieved near-GPT-4 performance for just $6M in training costs, a fraction of what frontier labs spend.
